What is Drawabox

Drawabox is a free course that focuses on teaching students the skills they need to draw from their imagination. Not just copying things directly from reference images where those references make all the decisions for you, but actually taking control over what it is you wish to depict, and using those references as tools that provide information that you can choose to use, alter, or ignore. If you would like a general breakdown of how the course is designed, the roadmap presented towards the beginning of Lesson 0 goes over this in some detail.

Read more...

Many come to believe that drawing in this manner is a "gift" or a "talent" - but that is not the case. It's simply a set of skills that can be developed. Not easily, and not quickly - this is difficult, challenging stuff - but it is not something out of reach of the average person.

Is Drawabox just drawing a bunch of boxes?

No, not at all, and when people describe it as such, it's a good sign that they may not be as familiar with the course as they claim to be. While this course is well known for its "250 box challenge" (which itself is not about drawing boxes any which way, but rather a very specific exercise), the course itself delves into drawing actual things like plants, insects, animals, and vehicles.

Read more...

At its core, this is a course about developing students' spatial reasoning skills, and we do that through constructional drawing exercises from lessons 3-7, which force students' brains through the process of solving 3D spatial puzzles by breaking down complex objects into combinations of simple forms, then building them back up on the page. Prior to that however, we ensure that students are armed with the ability to execute intentional, confident marks, a basic understanding of the principles of perspective, and the ability to roughly estimate the convergences of the lines they draw, so they can make the most out of those constructional drawing exercises.

How do I use this blasted website?

Lesson 0, which consists of 4 pages, is where you start going through the material. When videos come up (denoted by video thumbnails that have a little "Play" icon superimposed on top), watch the video, then resume going through the written material. Lessons consist of multiple pages, and at the bottom of each page, you will find links to the previous/next pages. Whenever homework comes up (the first time you'll encounter this is at the end of the Lines section in Lesson 1), complete the assigned quantity of pages for each exercise (you will find links to the exercise instructions here as well).

Is Drawabox free?

All of our lesson material is available for free on our website and on Youtube, no sign-in required. We also operate a very large community discord chat server where students help each other out on a voluntary basis.

Read more...

In addition to this, we operate our "official critique" program which allows students to access more reliable, timely feedback from our teaching assistants very cheaply (the cost of feedback for a lesson's homework ranges from around $5 to $10 USD - although this is subsidized by other students and sponsors who support Drawabox, and does not cover the full cost of the TAs who provide the feedback).

Will Drawabox teach me all I need to know to draw?

No. Drawabox is not a one-stop-shop that will teach you everything involved in drawing. We don't teach anything to do with drawing the human body, and even the topics you will engage with throughout the course (plants, insects, animals, vehicles, etc.) are used simply as a lens through which to look at the same concept, as opposed to teaching you all there is to know about drawing that topic.

Read more...

Drawabox sets out to teach students one thing - spatial reasoning - and everything else we end up learning along the way (for example, the rules of perspective, and how to execute accurate marks with confidence) are a biproduct of working towards that goal.

That said, what we do teach - spatial reasoning - is an extremely valuable skill, and is one that plays an outsized role in many of the other topics there are to learn. There are a lot of free and cheap resources out there across the internet, but you will find that as your spatial reasoning skills develop, what you're able to get out of such other resources will increase, and that the things they discuss will start to make more sense, giving you a greater capacity to apply what they teach and share.

What is spatial reasoning?

In the context of drawing, spatial reasoning refers to the subconscious understanding of the relationships between what you draw on a flat page, and what it tells us about the 3D structures it is meant to represent. To put it more simply, it's the skill that lets you draw things that are believably 3D without having to actually think about it.

Read more...

It is subconscious similarly to how when we walk, we rely on the subconscious parts of our brains to manage all of the specific movements of our leg muscles, while our conscious brain only focuses on where we wish to go. Similarly, an artist with strong spatial reasoning skills is able to focus their conscious cognitive resources on what it is they wish to draw (in the sense of design, composition, narrative - the creative things that actually make what we draw interesting) and rely on their subconscious spatial reasoning skills to influence how they draw their marks to capture how they sit in 3D space accurately (or at least close enough).

Many students operate under the assumption that drawing is either a very conscious activity (like solving a math problem), or that it is a magical activity (like watching someone juggle, if you've never juggled before), but neither is true. Rather, drawing is like a sport - we can do focused, targeted exercises to rewire our subconscious auto-pilot and train it to take care of certain tasks reliably, so that when we're out on the field, we can focus on the larger strategies being put into motion instead.

Do I need to read, or can I just watch the videos?

You should definitely go through both the written material and the videos - ideally by following along on the website, and watching videos when they come up. There is repetition, although the overlap between the two mediums is not perfect. Some things are better conveyed through video, others through text/diagrams, but regardless of that, absorbing the same information in different formats tends to help the brain understand and retain it better. Don't forget that the website will read the lesson material to you if that makes things easier.

I want to be a digital artist, can I do Drawabox digitally?

While many of our students are aiming to work digitally (and I myself have done all of my professional work digitally for as long as I can remember), as we explain here we have found that going through this course digitally tends to make it less effective for students, and tends to result in students rushing and generally being sloppier in ways that severely undermines what they get out of their work in this course.

Read more...

It's just not a good pairing of tools, and while we can't force students not to do the work digitally (all the material is freely available, so we can't stop you from using it in that way, nor do we care to), it is worth taking into consideration why we don't allow students to submit digital work for official critique - it increases our workload because those students more frequently run into problems from rushing, losing focus, or running into other issues that simply don't occur with the same frequency when working with the recommended tools.

Ultimately we recommend fineliners (as discussed here) because they align very well with the specific principles and concepts we seek to teach. When the things a given course focuses on and the medium it uses for its exercises align very well, it has an extremely beneficial effect on what students get for the time they put in, and the permanency of ink does a great job of aligning with how our course relies on hyper-intentional decision making behind every stroke, while also turning every mistake into a valuable learning opportunity.

That's not to say that ink is great for learning everything. For example, rather than using ink if a student was to learn figure drawing, I'd probably lean more into a graphite pencil whose lead has been further exposed with a utility knife to allow for a wider variety of mark-making surfaces (we talk about this a bit in this section from Lesson 1 where we discuss the different grips we use for different tools).

How do I know if my pen is a fineliner or not?

Take a close look at the tip of your pen, and compare it to the closeup image available in this section from the lesson material. Fineliners look pretty distinct from other kinds of pens, and so if yours' tip looks like this, then it is a fineliner. If it does not, then it probably is not a fineliner.

What are my options for getting feedback?

We don't want students working in a vacuum (trying to rely on their own judgment of their work) since they aren't generally going to be in a position to distinguish between what issues are entirely normal to encounter at the stage they're at, and which ones suggest misunderstandings of the material that need to be addressed - so, we've gone to considerable lengths to provide different options for receiving feedback. The main options include free community feedback and paid official critique.

Read more...

Free community feedback is,

  • As the name suggests, free of cost

  • Can be received both by submitting directly on the website (complete work only) and on our discord chat server (partial work is permitted, as our lesson-specific channels are better suited to it)

  • Does not have any hard requirements in terms of what tools to use (so you are allowed to submit digital work) but it relies on students volunteering their time, so some individuals may prefer to spend their time focusing on those who do adhere to the course recommendations (especially since those recommendations tend to result in providing that feedback being easier)

  • Does not guarantee that you will receive a response, or that it will be timely - again, it's based on volunteers. There is however an unofficial critique-exchange program that students operate on our discord chat server (specifically in the #critique-exchange channel) where by adhering to the specific structure and rules of that program (all of which is explained in that channel's pinned message) you can vastly increase your chances of getting feedback, especially for the earlier lessons

Paid official critique is,

  • Paid, although we offer it as cheaply as possible, charging students close to half of what our TAs are paid to provide that feedback, with the rest being covered by those community members who allow credits to expire

  • Received by paying for credits (currently via Patreon), connecting your Drawabox account to Patreon via the account settings page and redeeming them when submitting your work for critique. Credits expire 2 months after being received, at which point that money spent goes towards covering feedback that is given to students, and running Drawabox as a whole

  • Is only received by submitting directly on the website, with the "Submit for Official Critique" option

  • Only complete work for an entire lesson or challenge is accepted

  • Is handled by our trained teaching assistants, under my (Uncomfortable's) supervision, although I handle some critiques myself

  • Subject to stricter requirements, both in terms of what tools are used, and that students act in good faith, ensuring that they are giving themselves as much time as they require to do it to the best of their current ability (both in going through the instructions and in doing the work without rushing, aiming for arbitrary deadlines, etc)

  • Feedback generally comes back within 5 days or so (Lesson 1 is closer to 2 days, it varies from lesson to lesson)

You will find more specific information about the requirements for official critique here in Lesson 0.

I signed up for official critique on Patreon, what now?

In order for our system to know which Patreon account is yours, you will have to head over to the Drawabox Account Settings page, and under the "Connected Accounts" section, connect your Patreon. This will allow you to access your credits and spend them on receiving feedback.

Can I move from community feedback to official critique later?

As explained here, you essentially have until you've completed your Lesson 1 work to decide whether you want to pursue free community feedback or paid official critique without having to redo any work. After that point, due to the requirement of official critique that only permits students to move onto the next lesson/challenge once its prerequisites have been marked as complete via official critique, redoing some or all of your work becomes necessary to ensure that you've had the opportunity to apply the feedback you've received, keeping our TAs from having to rehash the same issues or have it distract from other issues that may be present.

Official critique involves a 14 day cooldown - what if I finish my work early?

If you finish your work before the 14 day cooldown has elapsed - which is unlikely to happen given how intensive the work can be - then you should ask yourself whether you appropriately adhered to the 50% rule, in spending at least as much time as you did on studies, on drawing as play. If you did not, then that is an excellent opportunity to do so as you wait for that cooldown to elapse. If you have, well, you can certainly play more (as the requirement is that you spend at least half of your time on play). Though you may also want to reflect on whether or not you invested as much time into absorbing and applying the instructions to the best of your current ability, as that can be a sign that you may have rushed.

Should I focus only on Drawabox, or is taking other courses okay?

I am a big proponent of students taking multiple courses at the same time, and I believe there is a lot of benefit to doing so, even if some of those courses would ostensibly benefit from a stronger grasp of what we teach here. Primarily, I find that it provides students with a more well rounded, big-picture understanding of how learning to draw works and the many components that play a role in working towards their own particular goals. There are however a few simple considerations one should keep in mind when doing so.

Read more...

  • You need to maintain reasonable expectations, and avoid comparing what you get from a given course with others who are taking it alongside you. It is natural that you will not gain as much in the short term in terms of what can readily be applied to your drawings, and your exercise work within that course may not meet the same standards as your peers due to prerequisite skills not being developed as far. Taken out of context, this can be very demotivating and frustrating, but going in with an awareness of it can help neutralize those negative emotions. Instead, focus on what it is you do get out of it, and how it fleshes out your overall understanding of how you're working towards your goals. Additionally, keep in mind that what you learn in any given course isn't merely about the short term benefits - as your other skills grow, you will find yourself being able to bring the concepts you've learned to bear, as long as you keep up with the exercises you've learned.

  • Similarly to the previous point, remember that you only have so much time to commit towards your efforts to learn to draw, and the more individual courses you follow at the same time, the less time each will receive (resulting in each course taking longer to complete). That is not a bad thing, but you should keep an eye on whether or not you're hitting a point where one of those courses ends up progressing at such a slow crawl that it's losing its purpose. Make sure that you can give each course enough time for it to individually be impactful, even stretched over a longer period of time, and do not forget to adhere to the 50% rule, which requires that for all of the time you spend studying, you should be spending at least as much time on drawing for the sake of drawing (drawing as play).

  • Every course uses its own strategies and methodologies for teaching its concepts, and different courses may seem to contradict one another. For example, Drawabox is very stringent about every mark needing to be the result of specific intent and planning, but another course may prefer you to build up your marks with multiple strokes, and to be more exploratory in "finding" the mark you wish to make. This does not mean that one is correct, and the other is wrong - rather, each one is presented in the larger context of the courses themselves, and it all comes down to how they play towards each course's individual goals. If a given course tells you to do something one way, make sure that you are applying that consistently for all of its work. Don't bring in approaches from other courses if they directly contradict the given instructions.

When it comes to the benefits of taking multiple courses, I think this is best explained with a metaphor. If your pursuit of these goals were to be framed as solving a jigsaw puzzle, working only from one course at a time is like starting from a single arbitrary piece, and only ever accepting pieces that connect to it. This kind of approach can generally be quite slow, especially early on, although it starts to speed up as you expand out from that singular point. It will also be a very long time before you really start to see the picture of the whole jigsaw puzzle emerge.

Conversely, if you were to start solving the puzzle from many different locations - in other words, by studying multiple subjects through different courses in parallel - this allows you to not simply expand out from one point but rather to bridge across from multiple starting points, closing off whole regions of the puzzle more quickly, even if there are still large gaps in between them. This kind of approach will help you to see the whole picture much sooner, as your brain will be able to fill in some of the gaps and infer information based on the larger framework being created. You'll develop a better grasp of how the different areas interconnect and feed off one another, and at least when it comes to learning, these are all greatly beneficial for both helping students to pick up skills more quickly, and to better understand what it is they're working towards.

So, if you have the time and resources to allow for it, I would strongly encourage you to explore different courses, rather than limiting yourself to completing them one by one in sequence.

I want to draw anime (or another specific style), will Drawabox help me?

While this course does not teach any specific style, every style of representational drawing (that is, drawing things that exist in the 3D world around us, or that could exist in the sense of fantastical things like dragons that still very much occupy 3D space) benefits immensely from an understanding of the relationship between the marks we draw on the page, and the 3D structures they are meant to represent. That is precisely what this course focuses on - so yes, Drawabox will help, but you will still have to study the particular style you're interested in as well. The video from this section goes into the relationship between the fundamentals we teach, and stylization, in more detail.

I don't know how to draw - how can I "play" per the 50% rule?

Anyone can play - but that doesn't mean it's going to be fun, or gratifying, or make you feel good about yourself. More than likely it'll require you to face the reality that you don't know what things look like, that your memory isn't very effective, and that everything you try to draw falls far short of what how you'd like for it to turn out. It can be extremely frustrating, and it is for most students at first - but facing that frustration is entirely what we're asking you to do, nothing more.

Read more...

Students ask this question for a number of reasons, but the main ones are that:

  • Either the student at some level still thinks that they need to be at some arbitrary level of skill, or have some arbitrary quality of a result, in order for them to draw without feeling shame, embarrassment, or just the sense that they've wasted their time, or

  • They associate "play" with an implication that it must be a fun, positive experience, and that if it is not, they must be doing it wrong

What we ask students to do with the 50% rule is not fun. It's not pleasant. At least, not initially, because most students have come to Drawabox specifically to escape from what they perceive as their inadequacies. But the first thing, the most important thing, for a student to do is to come to terms with the fact that what they perceive as an inadequacy is simply a fact of life, of being, of who they are. That they are not shameful because they don't have a skill they've never practiced - something that applies also to those who've spent years trying to learn to draw (I did this myself for a decade), because drawing is not a singular skill, but a vast collection of different skills, many of which can be important to our goals, but in a way that is never properly conveyed to us and thus never meaningfully developed.

A lot of students develop the impression that the 50% rule is a nice-to-have, a moment of rare compassion from the box demon for those weaker ones who can't simply sit and grind away at their exercises day in, day out. But that is about as far from the truth as you can get, and moreover, it's a lie they tell themselves because the idea of drawing as play genuinely frightens them. They want to stay in the warm, comforting embrace of exercises that promise them that their time won't be wasted, that even if it turns out badly, it's working towards something concrete and tangible.

But it's not. It is a fundamental component in the tug-of-war that is learning. The exercises we perform throughout this course involve being hyper-intentional at every turn, ensuring that nothing you do is the result of a reflexive action or a thoughtless choice. Everything, insofar as we can make it so, should be the result of a conscious choice. It's slow, it's time consuming, but it's necessary to ensure that the auto-pilot we are trying to train (the thing that decides how we draw when we aren't making those hyper-intentional choices) is trained reliably, so that it can perform correctly when we need it to.

The trouble with that is that the very process involved causes us to erode the trust we have in the auto-pilot. After all, if you spend weeks and months telling yourself that the auto-pilot is not to be trusted, so how exactly would you expect yourself to suddenly start trusting it when it comes time to finally draw your own stuff? It's not a switch you can flip - you have to maintain that balance the whole way through.

And so, while there are many reasons we push the 50% rule, one of the biggest is that it pushes back against this erosion of trust, and ensures that as your skills do develop, that they can have a direct impact on your own drawings. That when you come out the other side, you won't find yourself needlessly bogging your brain down trying to plan out every little mark, and will actually be able to focus on the creative decisions of design, composition, and narrative - the things that make our drawings interesting - and will be able to rely on your autopilot to take care of the rest.

I have no idea what to draw for my play portion of the 50% rule

While it is very common for students to automatically throw away the beginnings of ideas they may have based on them being too difficult to pull off to a particular standard (and this is something you should push back against, working to pay attention to the ideas you develop), we do have our own drawing prompts which you'll find here. Each one is detailed but open-ended - feel free to take them in whatever direction you wish, as long as you actually draw something, regardless of how it turns out.

Can I use reference when playing per the 50% rule?

You may use reference as a source of information, but you must be making compositional and design choices yourself. Simply copying something from reference, or from life, is a study and falls under the "study" half of the 50% rule. A good way to avoid this is to first do a rough sketch without reference to make the general decisions for whatever it is you're drawing, then use that sketch to determine what kind of references to bring in when redrawing it a second time. You'll find more information on this in the dedicated FAQ section for the 50% rule.

Should I consciously apply what I've learned when playing per the 50% rule?

No, when drawing as play, you should be drawing in whatever way you find to be natural and comfortable. The intent of the work we do in the course is to gradually rewire how you draw naturally, so what you learn from the course may start changing how it is you draw without thinking about it, but you should not be spending your conscious mind's cognitive resources on thinking about how to approach your drawing. It should be focused on making creative decisions instead. You'll find more information on this in the dedicated FAQ section for the 50% rule.

I'm afraid that drawing as play will result in bad habits

It's very common for students to be concerned about developing bad habits, but this is deeply misguided and will interfere with your growth. You will develop bad habits. Sometimes they won't even start out as bad habits, but rather will be useful, but as your skills develop, will end up holding you back. It is entirely normal to have to unlearn behaviours, and you will do so frequently across the process. If however you avoid things out of a fear of developing bad habits, you will severely limit yourself. After all, another word for "bad habit" is "crutch" - and when your leg's broken, you'll be happy you had crutches to rely upon.

Where do portfolio pieces fit into the 50% rule?

By default, the 50% rule doesn't factor in portfolio pieces, but in the dedicated FAQ section for the 50% rule there are some suggestions on how that can be incorporated. Keep in mind that if you're a complete beginner, portfolio pieces (whose purpose is to demonstrate the best of which you're capable, often for the purposes of trying to court clients or employers) is not something you should be worrying about. This doesn't mean you shouldn't or can't aim to create whole finished pieces - just that the intent of a "portfolio" piece goes beyond that.

Why does official critique use a weird "credit" system?

Our use of our credit system allows us to provide official critique with a lot of flexibility in terms of how much it actually ends up costing students, by allowing both the option for those students with tighter budgets to only sign up to get credits when they need them (right before submitting their homework), and cancel/pause their subscription in between, while those with more flexibility in their budgets can maintain an ongoing subscription and potentially allow some credits to expire. Without any credits expiring (which they all do naturally 2 months after being received if they aren't spent), we cover up to half the cost of the teaching assistants who provide the feedback (so the service can be accessed more cheaply), and those credits that are allowed to expire go towards balancing those scales.

Read more...

While a simpler exchange of a clear, consistent price per critique without the credit system would be much less complicated, it would require us to charge more than twice as much as we do currently, making the service inaccessible for many of our students, and undermining one of our core operating principles of prioritizing the financial accessibility of what we offer. Instead, while our current system is riskier for us (since we regularly have to monitor just how many credits are spent versus how many are permitted to expire, and normally positive things like a sudden influx of new users can actually greatly increase our operating costs by causing more credits to be spent in the short term), this approach allows us to strike that precarious balance to ensure our students are getting as much as they can, while not being pushed to spend more than they're able.

In this manner, Drawabox is very much a communal effort, where the community as a whole helps to ensure that our TAs are paid well for their time and experience, and that those students who are more restricted in their finances are still able to access reliable feedback.

Learning to draw should be fun - why isn't Drawabox?

Above all else, our goal is to provide students with as much as we can, while asking for as little as possible in return, and this puts us in the precarious position of doing so with extremely limited resources. In order for this to be feasible, we have had to make clear decisions on what it is we need to take responsibility for, and what we must rely on students to manage for themselves. A big part of that is a student's own motivation, which has resulted in many finding Drawabox to be decidedly un-fun. There is certainly merit to that, but it is not at all that we believe drawing should be a purely academic endeavor (nothing can be farther from the truth, given what we stress about the importance of play in our 50% rule.

Read more...

As a business, we operate on a few core tenets - primarily that we prioritize the financial accessibility (keep things free where we can, and cheap where free is not an option), and that we pay our teaching assistants fairly for their time and expertise, two things that exist very much at odds with one another. We have miraculously been able to achieve these goals due to the generosity of our community (though the base price of feedback does not cover the cost of having a TA provide it, enough students allow their credits to expire to balance those scales), and the very specific requirements we attach to receiving official critique (around which the entire course is intentionally designed).

Having students take responsibility for whatever they can, primarily in terms of being responsible for their own motivation, and in being willing to invest the time necessary to ensure that the work they submit is the best of which they are currently capable (so it can accurately represent their current understanding, allowing our TAs to provide feedback and next steps that best serve the student in question - something that cannot happen if a student rushes their work) allows us to use our TAs' time as efficiently as possible, keeping those costs down.

If we were to take on more responsibilities - for example, providing students with more opportunities for feedback within a given lesson, instead of just at the end, to reduce their fears of carrying mistakes forward (something that would double and triple our TAs' workload, since providing feedback on partial work takes about as much time as it does for completed work) - then our costs would balloon resulting in official critique no longer being something we could provide as cheaply as we do.

While we would love to see a future in which we have enough resources to bring to bear to serve our students better, right now this is simply the compromise we are forced to make to ensure that those who are more financially limited are not left behind.

Why is Drawabox so grindy?

The answer to this is similar to the one given for why is drawabox not fun, in that a big contributing factor to the "grindiness" of Drawabox is rooted in the limitations under which we're operating by being free. We're working with students of all different kinds, and where some students may achieve the desired impact of a given task with a far lighter workload, others will require significantly more - and in order to work within our resource limitations, we have to leave it to the students to bear the brunt of the extra work so as to limit how much we call on our teaching assistants to review the work itself. It's a simple trade-off of the fact that we prioritize financial accessibility.

Read more...

An argument certainly can be made for a pass/fail assessment with no actual feedback that would undoubtedly incur less overhead, but that approach has never sat right with me. After all, having someone tell me that my work isn't "quite there yet" but not being able to give me any actionable advice on how to deal with that would be deeply frustrating due to the lack of clarity involved - not worth the additional (albeit smaller) expense required to provide it.

This actually aligns with the reason why we don't believe Drawabox is actually "grindy" - at least, not in the way we define the word ourselves (convenient, I know). We believe that grinding is what we do when we continually repeat the same task with no solid, predefined point at which we should stop. An example of this would be drawing boxes over and over until you are satisfied with the result. The metric being your own satisfaction makes it very untrustworthy - it's a goalpost that will shift based on how you feel, and the better you get at drawing those boxes, the more attuned you'll be to noticing your own mistakes - further pushing down your satisfaction and keeping the grind going.

Conversely, if given a task to draw 250 boxes in a particular way, and being done when you hit 250 regardless of how you feel about them - that has a clearly defined end point that doesn't move. Every box you draw is another step towards that goalpost, and you can trust that it won't run from you to artificially extend your torment.

Grinding is not a good thing, it leans into our worst tendencies and feeds on a lack of self-confidence. A large task, however, is not inherently a case of grinding - sometimes things involve a lot of steps and repetition, but as long as they have a clear end, you will get there, one step at a time.

Drawabox is not for me - is there no hope?

Drawabox is just one approach to learning to draw, one of many. Plenty of students ultimately decide that the way we approach teaching isn't a good fit for them - whether in general, or at this point in their lives - and that is perfectly fine. It does not in any way mean that such students can't learn to draw, or that other approaches won't work better for you as you are right now.

Read more...

Our approach is a rigorous one, and one with a specific set of goals. There are many things - for example, observational skills - that we simply don't cover super deeply, that other courses will put more focus on - and conversely, the spatial reasoning we do focus on are things that other courses won't put as much focus on, allowing it to develop more as a side effect of what they do teach. That doesn't mean one course is better than another, it simply means that there are many ways to get started, and that these are all different paths pointing in the same general direction.

It is also worth keeping in mind that not all students use Drawabox in the same way. It is entirely normal for our students to feel they've gotten what they wanted out of it and move onto other resources. Some of them come back after a while once they feel that they're at a point where they'd like to continue along our path, and others are perfectly happy to continue exploring the world of resources out there. Drawabox is not a course where the only way to benefit from it is to complete the course from start to finish, or that this needs to be done in one go.

So relax. Take whatever good we were able to share with you, and start looking for the next resource to continue on your journey - or just spend some time drawing as play. That said, if you do find yourself trying a lot of resources and dropping out of them quickly, this comic on the subject may grant you some perspective. It talks about how instead of trying to gauge how good of a fit a given resource is with every step, to focus instead on whether or not you trust in the resource or the instructor behind it. If you can continue to trust their guidance, continue to make the best effort to follow their directions as closely as you can, with as little alteration as possible. If however you cross that threshold where you're no longer able to trust them, that's okay - put the resource down, take what good you got from it, and move onto something else.

I took a long break from Drawabox, how do I resume where I was?

While everyone's different, between being away for different periods of time, some peoples' memory of the material being better than others, and some students keeping up with their warmup exercises even while being away, while others not having touched the Drawabox material at all for a long stretch, here's our basic rule of thumb for those returning to Drawabox after a long break:

Read more...

  • Firstly, review all of the material up to the point where you took your break, starting from Lesson 0. This is to ensure you're up to date on everything and haven't forgotten anything critical, either in terms of the course material or the specific instructions associated with each exercise.

  • Secondly, for a stretch of time - I'd do 1 week for every year you've been away, so if you've been away for half a year, then make it half a week - spend the time you would generally allocate for your studies exclusively on exercises from your warmup pool. If for whatever reason 1 week per year away isn't sufficient to revisit each exercise to some degree, then give yourself however much time that would require, with the goal being that you're at least exposing yourself to each exercise to some degree.

  • Finally, resume where you left off.

Getting Your Work Critiqued

Having your work reviewed by others is critical, as those who are just starting out aren't in a position to properly judge their own work, and won't be for quite some time. Don't be afraid to show your struggles - it's by analyzing your mistakes that we can help you grow. Perfect homework is not what we're looking for; we just need it to be complete.

There are currently two places you can get your work critiqued by the community - Reddit and our Discord Chat Server.

Both of these are completely free.

Private Patreon Critiques

If you are interested in receiving extra help, I critique the work of those who support Drawabox on Patreon.

All of these private critiques are done through reddit, in specific threads where students post their work as a comment, including a link to their work (often hosted on Imgur, though most image hosts are okay).

  • All work for the lesson must be completed - that means all exercises in the lesson, not just those in a given section
