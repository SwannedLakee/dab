Frequently Asked Questions

This page lists many different frequently asked questions related to this lesson, however you can use the Main FAQ page which includes a search function to more easily find information on specific topics, across all the lesson FAQs that are available.

Can I draw with my elbow instead of my shoulder?

In this course we specifically avoid driving the drawing motion from the elbow, and focus on using either the shoulder (which covers most cases) or the wrist (on occasion). It's true, drawing with your whole arm from the shoulder is difficult, and that there are many marks that will be just fine being drawn from your elbow - but what we're doing here is not about the marks you can make today, right now. We are not sharing with you tips and tricks whose goal is to produce immediate results. Our process is one that involves addressing key weaknesses that hold students back - and being able to draw using the whole arm from the shoulder is one of them.

Read more...

You'll find more information on this here and here in the lesson material, but the short answer is outside of disabilities and other circumstances that may be unique to you (which only you will have the information to navigate for yourself), **you should be striving to execute any lines, big or small, that require a smooth consistent flow, from your shoulder.

Can I draw lines from my wrist if they're really small?

It is not the size of the line that dictates which pivot of your arm will best suit making the mark, it is the nature of that mark you're trying to make. A lot of textural details for instance (sometimes you won't encounter until Lesson 2) benefit more from the kind of tight control that working from your wrist, or even your fingers, can provide - just like handwriting. But the majority of the marks we draw in this course (and all of those you'll be drawing throughout Lesson 1, the box challenge, and so forth) require smooth, confident executions to maintain consistent trajectories, even your ellipses, and as such you should be engaging your whole arm from the shoulder.

Read more...

While you're still getting used to using your whole arm from the shoulder, this may result in a negative impact on your results, but as you get more comfortable with it, you'll find that it actually serves as a stabilizer, allowing you to maintain confidence in your drawing motions at slower speeds, allowing you to in turn regain more control.

You'll find more info on this in this section.

How should I hold my pen?

In this course, you'll be holding your pen in the same way you do to write, although try to maintain a relaxed grip - don't squeeze your pen tight. You'll find more information on this - specifically why we hold our pen in this manner, and why other courses and tools might use different grips, in this section.

Do I need to draw with my hand hovering over the page?

No, you can feel free to rest the side of your hand or your pinky on the page for stability, and in fact we recommend this in most cases. You'll find this addressed in more detail in this section.

Is it okay if I do my homework on the bus, laying on my bed, on the couch, or in other similarly less-than-stable positions?

Probably not a good idea. Anything that keeps you from being in a focused, studious mindset should be avoided. This includes unstable drawing positions, busy/crowded/noisy spaces, messy environments, drawing on lined or graph paper, and so on. You absolutely want to put your best effort into completing the exercises, as that is the way you're going to get the most out of them. Being in a less than optimal mindset will cause you to work sloppier, which is something you want to avoid at all costs. If you don't have any kind of a reasonable table or desk at home, consider going out to a public library instead.

Read more...

Keep in mind that we want to be as intentional and meticulous in applying all of the methodologies and strategies we introduce throughout the course, and it will be very tempting to rely more and more on your instincts or auto-pilot as you progress through the course, and the kind of situations that leave us less focused and less stable will lean into that. The goal for what we're doing in this course is essentially training that auto-pilot to make it more reliable, so that when you draw outside of this course, you can rely on it to do all of the heavy lifting, freeing your brain up for the creative decisions of composition, design, and narrative which actually make your work special. But to get to that point, we have to make sure that as we do the work assigned here, we're giving it our all.

If the instructions want me to do something one way, but a different way is easier for me, can I do it that way instead?

No, you should not be modifying the instructions (at least not outside of situations involving disabilities, in which case you should still try and adhere as closely to those instructions as you can). If the instructions for an exercise ask you to tackle something in a particular manner, then that approach is very much part of the exercise, and to change them (especially changing them based on what'll get you better short term results) is more than likely going to alter the exercise itself away from what it was designed to achieve.

Read more...

Furthermore, just having your results turn out badly doesn't mean that you weren't following the exercise as intended - the short term results aren't always going to help you understand whether you're doing things right, as you're not in a position to differentiate between things that are signs of misunderstanding, and issues that are entirely normal and expected. That's why students have just one job: follow the instructions as closely as they are able.

My fineliners keep dying!

Setting aside the variance between different brands of pens, a very common experience amongst students starting out with the course is that their pens die quickly. The reason is the lack of pressure control - as a beginner, you don't yet have the mileage and experience to vary how much pressure you're applying, and so you're probably gonna be pushing that nib into the page pretty hard. This damages the nib and impedes ink flow, causing your pen to seem like it's running low on ink even when there's plenty available. Unfortunately the outcome is the same, as most fineliners don't have replaceable nibs. Fortunately, this will improve with practice. In the meantime, you may want to buy cheaper pens initially, then invest in more expensive ones once you're able to take advantage of them.

My homework looks terrible, can I redo it?

That is not a decision for you to make. Instead, you focus on giving yourself enough time to absorb, follow, and apply the instructions to the best of your current ability, so as to create a body of work that shows whoever is giving you feedback what it is you understand, what you might need more help with, and what next steps to recommend. It is not your responsibility to judge whether or not your work looks terrible. All you're in charge of is giving yourself enough time.

Read more...

Of course, there's always going to be situations where you realize after the fact that you rushed, but covering up that poor judgment by redoing the page isn't going to make you less likely to do it again. Rather, having someone else call you out for that choice will sting, but it'll also make you more likely to avoid that kind of decision in the future.

Above all, keep in mind that the homework you're doing here are just exercises. They're not works of art, and so there's no reason to be overly concerned with how they turn out right now. It's the doing of the exercise that is valuable, and how that work turned out will contain valuable information on how to ensure that you get the most out of that kind of work going forward, and that valuable information can be understood and relayed to you by students and instructors who've had more experience.

I do not understand some of the theoretical lectures from the material, is that a problem?

Honestly - and I'm being completely genuine here - it's not a big deal. What students learn from this course all comes from the exercises, from following those instructions as intentionally and purposely as possible, getting their understanding of those instructions confirmed through feedback, and then continuing to apply them in their regular warmups throughout the rest of the course (and even beyond). The theoretical concepts only serve to get students to a point where they're willing to try the exercises.

Read more...

Some students are perfectly happy to dive right in, while others find themselves mentally blocked by a billion questions and will only attempt the exercises once those questions have been answered to some degree. Since we're catering to a wide variety of students to the best of our ability, we provide additional theory where we can, but only in the hopes of getting more students to the exercises.

Ultimately anything you might need to understand that you don't quite yet grasp should be caught when you submit your work for feedback, so remember - as long as you're not working in a vacuum, you're going to be okay. You just need to take our instructions at face value (especially with disclaimers like those included in the video where we discuss the concept of infinity in the boxes section), and not assume that we're just trying to be nice but actually lying to you.

Some of these exercises are impossible, how am I supposed to progress?

You probably just hit the rotated boxes exercise, and are having second thoughts about this course. I get it. The thing is, we intentionally assign exercises at times that we simply do not expect students to be able to tackle successfully, and the rotated boxes (as well as the organic perspective boxes that come afterwards) are good examples of this. We use them to introduce students to a problem - the idea of rotating boxes arbitrarily in space, without necessarily having a specific perspective grid and clearly defined vanishing points to rely upon - before we actually delve into material that will help you tackle it more successfully. So you may be struggling with it, but that doesn't mean you're not progressing as expected.

Read more...

This approach of introducing problems first to provide context as to what it is we're dealing with, then working on breaking that problem down a little later, allows our course to operate more efficiently both for the student and in terms of providing feedback (whether that's from our staff for official critique, or from other students in community feedback). It simply requires that the student set aside their ego, and the idea that failing to successfully execute an exercise according to their arbitrary expectations is somehow beneath them.

The work we assign in this course has no expectations that students will complete it perfectly, or even well. That's not its purpose. Its purpose is to provide whoever's then giving feedback a body of work that shows what the student does understand (even if they don't necessarily have the skills to execute it exactly as they intend right now), what they don't yet grasp and might need clarification on, and what next steps will be in their best interest. It is a tool to help the person giving you feedback, and so the only thing that matters is that you give yourself as much time as you need to absorb the instructions and follow them to the best of your current ability - because it's that "current ability" and current understanding that we need to gauge to be able to help you.

My hand slipped and I made a mistake - can I correct it?

Do not correct your mistakes. Let them stand for themselves, as that is how they will provide the most educational value. When we correct mistakes, it tricks the brain into thinking that their cause has been addressed (whereas what was corrected was just the symptom), making it no less likely that you'll end up doing it again. It's better instead to leave your mistakes as they are (even if you're still halfway through a drawing and have to build upon that mistake), and even to have it called out by someone else in critique if they deem it necessary.

That ghosting method - do I have to use it everywhere?

Yup! Every mark you freehand throughout this course should apply the ghosting method. The ghosting method is important because it separates the mark making process into distinct stages, each with their own priorities, and so it reduces how much your brain has to focus on achieving at any given moment. This is a big concept throughout this course, the idea of dividing things up into stages so as to reduce the cognitive load and allocate more resources towards each individual task. You'll find more information about this in this section from the lesson material.

How do I apply line weight?

The use of line weight is discussed here in the rotated boxes material, since that's where we first meaningfully start dealing with overlaps. While outside of this course there are a lot of different ways one might choose to use line weight, in this course we are strictly adhering to only using line weight in the limited, localized areas where specific overlaps between lines need to be clarified, and we are focusing on keeping that line weight subtle - like a whisper to the viewer's subconscious, rather than an overt shout.

Do I have to draw through all of my ellipses?

Yes. Every ellipse you freehand throughout this course should be drawn through two full times before lifting your pen. While initially you may do a bit more than that, aim in the long run for two turns of the shape, no less, no more.

How do I get feedback on my homework?

This is discussed in more detail on this page of Lesson 0, with the submission process being explained in the video at the top of that page.

Typically students complete all of the work assigned for an entire lesson (which can contain multiple sections, so for example Lesson 1 has a Lines section, an Ellipses section, and a Boxes section) to the best of their current ability prior to submitting it for feedback, but they are permitted to ask questions and share partial work on our discord chat server, in the dedicated channel for the given lesson. Outside of that, both free community feedback and paid official critique is submitted through our website here, but again submissions on those platforms must be complete, containing all of the assigned work across the entire lesson.

I've signed up for official critique through Patreon - how do I get my credits?

To access credits you've gotten through Patreon, ensure that you have connected your Patreon account on the Drawabox account settings page. If you scroll down that page, you will see a section for connecting different third party accounts, the first one being Patreon. Once connected, you should see in that same section how many credits you have to spend.

When do these exercises become part of my warmup routine?

Once you have received feedback on your work confirming that you understand how they should be approached - which happens after you've completed the entire lesson - it can be added to the warmup routine where you pick two or three exercises to do for 10-15 minutes (total) at the beginning of each homework session. This means that until you've had your Lesson 1 work marked as complete (at which point you'll move onto the 250 box challenge), you will not have any warmups.

Getting Your Work Critiqued

Having your work reviewed by others is critical, as those who are just starting out aren't in a position to properly judge their own work, and won't be for quite some time. Don't be afraid to show your struggles - it's by analyzing your mistakes that we can help you grow. Perfect homework is not what we're looking for; we just need it to be complete.

There are currently two places you can get your work critiqued by the community - Reddit and our Discord Chat Server.

Both of these are completely free.

Private Patreon Critiques

If you are interested in receiving extra help, I critique the work of those who support Drawabox on Patreon.

All of these private critiques are done through reddit, in specific threads where students post their work as a comment, including a link to their work (often hosted on Imgur, though most image hosts are okay).

My requirements are more strict than the free community critiques:

  • You must complete the lessons in order, in their entirety, starting from lesson 1
  • All work for the lesson must be completed - that means all exercises in the lesson, not just those in a given section
  • You may only move onto the next lesson once the previous has been marked complete
  • The work must be done in the tools recommended in the lesson

Pledges are collected at the beginning of the following month, but you may start submitting your work immediately. If you're a new patron, I'll be reaching out to your shortly to collect your reddit username.
